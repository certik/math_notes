# Free-Particle Dynamics from Spacetime Symmetry

This note derives the motion of a massive free particle without choosing the
Galilean or Lorentzian branch at the start. We import the common one-parameter
kinematical algebra classified in [the spacetime note](17-spacetime.md), prove
inertial motion once for both causal branches, and specialize only when an
explicit action is needed. The logical order is

$$
\text{common physical principles}
\longrightarrow
\text{two spacetime branches}
\longrightarrow
\text{shared velocity-space symmetry}
\longrightarrow
\text{inertial motion}
\longrightarrow
\text{action principle}
\longrightarrow
\text{Lagrangian and Noether charges}.
$$

Passing to a curved spacetime requires an additional universal-free-fall
postulate and a specified connection. It does not follow from coordinate
covariance alone.

## Scope and assumptions

The result below concerns a structureless massive point particle with no
external fields.

**Physical principles**

1. No inertial frame is preferred, space is isotropic, and spacetime is
   homogeneous.
2. The free-particle law has the same geometric form under every connected
   inertial symmetry.
3. The spacetime classification of
   [the preceding note](17-spacetime.md) is imported: under its explicit
   strong-homogeneity, causality, and relativity assumptions, the admissible
   kinematics form the family $\mathfrak g_\kappa$ with
   $\kappa=0$ or $\kappa>0$. No branch is selected here.

**Mathematical assumptions**

1. The worldline is twice differentiable.
2. The law is local and second order: acceleration at an event depends only
   on the event and the velocity there. It has no dependence on higher
   derivatives, internal variables, or hidden background fields.
3. The massive worldline carries the invariant affine parameter supplied by
   the chosen branch: absolute time when $\kappa=0$ and proper time when
   $\kappa>0$. This parameter is preserved by the connected inertial group.

These assumptions define the class in which uniqueness is claimed. Symmetry
alone does not exclude higher-derivative laws, nonlocal laws, massless
particles, or particles carrying additional internal data.

The branch-specific spacetime tensors are derived in
[the metric note](13-poincare-galilei-metric.md), but the proof of inertial
motion below needs only the common group action on velocity space.

## Inertial motion

### Common velocity space

Let $\mathcal V_\kappa$ be the space of admissible future-directed inertial
velocity states in either causal branch, and choose a reference rest state
$u_0$. The spacetime classification establishes three facts common to both
branches:

1. the connected group $G_0$ generated by rotations and boosts acts
   transitively on $\mathcal V_\kappa$;
2. the stabilizer of $u_0$ is $SO(3)$;
3. $SO(3)$ acts on $T_{u_0}\mathcal V_\kappa$ as its ordinary
   three-dimensional vector representation.

Thus

$$
\mathcal V_\kappa=G_0/SO(3).
$$

This statement does not choose $\kappa=0$ or $\kappa>0$.

### General free-particle law

Let $s$ denote the branch's invariant affine parameter and define

$$
u=\frac{dx}{ds}\in\mathcal V_\kappa.
$$

Before imposing translations, the most general law in the stated local
second-order class is

$$
\frac{du}{ds}=F(x,u),
\qquad
F(x,u)\in T_u\mathcal V_\kappa.
$$

Spacetime-translation invariance removes the event dependence:

$$
\frac{du}{ds}=A(u).
$$

Therefore a candidate free law is a vector field
$A:\mathcal V_\kappa\to T\mathcal V_\kappa$.

### Equivariance forces the vector field to vanish

The relativity principle says that transforming a complete worldline by any
$g\in G_0$ produces another solution of the same law. On velocity space this
is the equivariance condition

$$
A(g\mathbin{\cdot}u)=g_*A(u),
$$

where $g_*$ is the induced map between tangent spaces.
Because $s$ is invariant, $du/ds$ transforms by this same tangent map; no
extra reparametrization factor appears.

Now restrict to a rotation $R$ in the stabilizer of $u_0$. Since
$R\mathbin{\cdot}u_0=u_0$, equivariance gives

$$
A(u_0)=A(R\mathbin{\cdot}u_0)=R_*A(u_0)
$$

for every $R\in SO(3)$. But
$T_{u_0}\mathcal V_\kappa$ is the ordinary spatial vector representation.
No nonzero vector is fixed by every spatial rotation. Hence

$$
A(u_0)=0.
$$

Finally, transitivity means that every $u\in\mathcal V_\kappa$ has the form
$u=g\mathbin{\cdot}u_0$ for some $g\in G_0$. A second use of equivariance
gives

$$
A(u)=A(g\mathbin{\cdot}u_0)=g_*A(u_0)=0.
$$

Thus both causal spacetime branches have the same unique free-particle law
within the stated class:

$$
\boxed{\frac{du}{ds}=0.}
$$

The proof used the full velocity-space orbit and its stabilizer, not an
explicit Galilean or Lorentz boost formula.

### Branch checks

For $\kappa=0$, the velocity space and affine parameter are

$$
\mathcal V_0
=\{u:\tau_\mu u^\mu=1\}
\cong\mathbb R^3,
\qquad
s=t.
$$

Writing $u=(1,\mathbf v)$, the common equation becomes

$$
\frac{d\mathbf v}{dt}=0.
$$

For $\kappa=1/c^2>0$,

$$
\mathcal V_{1/c^2}
=\{u:g(u,u)=-c^2,\ u\text{ future}\},
\qquad
s=\tau.
$$

The common equation is

$$
\frac{du^\mu}{d\tau}=a^\mu=0.
$$

These are coordinate realizations of the same invariant conclusion: the
velocity state is constant, so the worldline is an affinely parametrized
straight line.

## What has and has not been proved

Within the local second-order class above, the common homogeneous
velocity-space structure uniquely selects straight, affinely parametrized
worldlines in both branches. No branch-specific boost formula, action, or
momentum law was used.

This does **not** prove that the equation has a unique Lagrangian. Inverse
variational problems generally admit inequivalent Lagrangians producing the
same trajectories. To determine the familiar free-particle Lagrangians we
must add more input.

## Adding an action principle

Assume Hamilton's principle for a local first-order action,

$$
S=\int L(\mathbf x,\mathbf v,t)\,dt.
$$

We add the variational symmetry assumption that every inertial symmetry is a
quasi-symmetry of this action: it may change $L$ by a total time derivative.
This is stronger than covariance of the equation of motion and is not implied
by the inertial-motion derivation above.

Two Lagrangians differing by

$$
L' = L+\frac{d\chi(\mathbf x,t)}{dt}
$$

have the same Euler-Lagrange equations. Any uniqueness statement below is
therefore only modulo such a total derivative.

### Galilean Lagrangian

Choose a homogeneous and isotropic representative $L=L(\mathbf v)$. A
Galilean boost with infinitesimal parameter $\mathbf b$ acts by

$$
\delta\mathbf x=-\mathbf b\,t,
\qquad
\delta\mathbf v=-\mathbf b.
$$

The action need only be invariant up to an endpoint term. For each
infinitesimal boost, therefore, there may be functions
$f_i(\mathbf x,t)$ such that

$$
-\frac{\partial L}{\partial v_i}
=\frac{df_i}{dt}
=\frac{\partial f_i}{\partial t}
+v_j\frac{\partial f_i}{\partial x_j}.
$$

Differentiate with respect to $v_k$:

$$
-\frac{\partial^2L}{\partial v_i\partial v_k}
=\frac{\partial f_i}{\partial x_k}.
$$

The left-hand side depends only on $\mathbf v$, while the right-hand side
depends only on $(\mathbf x,t)$. Since the equality holds for every state,
both sides are a constant matrix. Rotation invariance permits only a scalar
multiple of $\delta_{ik}$, so for some constant $m$,

$$
\frac{\partial^2L}{\partial v_i\partial v_k}
=m\delta_{ik}.
$$

Integrating once gives

$$
\frac{\partial L}{\partial v_i}=m v_i+q_i.
$$

The constant vector $\mathbf q$ would select a preferred spatial direction,
so isotropy requires $\mathbf q=0$. A second integration yields

$$
L=\frac12m\mathbf v^2+C.
$$

The constant is dynamically irrelevant because $C=d(Ct)/dt$. Restoring the
general total-derivative freedom gives

$$
\boxed{
L_{\mathrm G}
=\frac12m\mathbf v^2+\frac{d\chi}{dt}.
}
$$

This proves the form of the action but leaves the coefficient $m$ free.
Positive kinetic stability selects $m>0$. The coefficient is the particle's
Bargmann central charge and will be called its mass. Momentum has not yet
been defined or used.

### Relativistic Lagrangian

For a structureless free particle, require the action to be local, additive
along adjacent worldline segments, reparametrization invariant, and Lorentz
invariant. Let $\lambda$ be an arbitrary increasing worldline parameter and
write

$$
S=\int \mathcal L(\dot x)\,d\lambda,
\qquad
\dot x^\mu=\frac{dx^\mu}{d\lambda}.
$$

Translation invariance removes explicit $x$-dependence. For a timelike
worldline, Lorentz invariance permits dependence on $\dot x^\mu$ only through

$$
z=-\eta_{\mu\nu}\dot x^\mu\dot x^\nu>0,
$$

so $\mathcal L(\dot x)=f(z)$. Under a constant reparametrization for which
$\dot x\mapsto k\dot x$, invariance of
$\mathcal L\,d\lambda$ requires positive homogeneity,

$$
f(k^2z)=k f(z)
$$

for every $k>0$. Taking $k=\sqrt{w/z}$ gives

$$
f(w)=\sqrt{\frac wz}\,f(z),
$$

so $f(z)=\beta\sqrt z$ for one constant $\beta$. Because
$\sqrt z\,d\lambda=c\,d\tau$, the action has the form

$$
S=\alpha\int d\tau
$$

with $\alpha=\beta c$, a constant having dimensions of energy. Thus locality,
Lorentz invariance, and reparametrization invariance fix the form; they do not
fix this particle-dependent normalization.

Using

$$
d\tau=dt\sqrt{1-\frac{\mathbf v^2}{c^2}},
$$

we obtain

$$
L=\alpha\sqrt{1-\frac{\mathbf v^2}{c^2}}.
$$

At small velocity,

$$
L
=\alpha-\frac{\alpha}{2c^2}\mathbf v^2
+O(\mathbf v^4/c^4).
$$

The constant $\alpha$ does not affect the non-relativistic equations of
motion. Requiring the quadratic term to approach the Galilean action with
the same mass parameter $m$ gives

$$
-\frac{\alpha}{2c^2}=\frac m2,
\qquad
\alpha=-mc^2.
$$

Equivalently, one may define the positive mass of this branch by
$m=-\alpha/c^2$; positivity of the low-velocity kinetic term selects the
minus sign. Thus

$$
\boxed{
L_{\mathrm P}
=-mc^2\sqrt{1-\frac{\mathbf v^2}{c^2}}
+\frac{d\chi}{dt}.
}
$$

This conclusion applies to massive timelike particles. No momentum law was
assumed. A massless particle
requires an auxiliary worldline field or an equivalent affine-parameter
action and is a separate branch.

## Equations of motion from the actions

For the Galilean action,

$$
\frac{d}{dt}\frac{\partial L_{\mathrm G}}{\partial\mathbf v}
=m\dot{\mathbf v}=0.
$$

For the relativistic action,

$$
\frac{d}{dt}\frac{\partial L_{\mathrm P}}{\partial\mathbf v}
=\frac{d}{dt}(\gamma m\mathbf v)=0.
$$

For $m>0$ and $|\mathbf v|<c$, the map
$\mathbf v\mapsto\partial L_{\mathrm P}/\partial\mathbf v
=\gamma m\mathbf v$ is one-to-one. Its conservation therefore implies
constant $\mathbf v$ and hence $a^\mu=0$. The actions reproduce, rather than
supply, the previously derived inertial motion.

## Noether charges

After the action principle has been introduced, Noether's theorem defines the
charges associated with spatial and temporal translations. Choosing the
homogeneous representatives $\chi=0$, spatial translations define momentum
by

$$
\mathbf p:=\frac{\partial L}{\partial\mathbf v},
$$

while time translations define energy by

$$
E:=\mathbf v\mathbin{\cdot}
\frac{\partial L}{\partial\mathbf v}-L.
$$

Evaluating these definitions gives

| Quantity | Galilean | Relativistic |
| --- | --- | --- |
| momentum | $\mathbf p=m\mathbf v$ | $\mathbf p=\gamma m\mathbf v$ |
| energy | $E=\frac12m\mathbf v^2$ | $E=\gamma mc^2$ |

For the relativistic particle,

$$
E
=\mathbf v\mathbin{\cdot}\frac{\partial L_{\mathrm P}}{\partial\mathbf v}
-L_{\mathrm P}
=\gamma mc^2.
$$

It follows algebraically that

$$
E^2-\mathbf p^2c^2=m^2c^4.
$$

In Galilean mechanics an additive constant in the energy remains
conventional. In the relativistic theory, $(E/c,\mathbf p)$ must transform as
a four-vector, so a frame-independent additive shift of $E$ is not compatible
with Lorentz covariance.

## Curved spacetime and the geodesic postulate

Coordinate covariance is a requirement on how an already specified geometric
law is represented; it is not a physical force law and does not determine a
connection.

To pass from inertial motion to gravity, add:

1. **universal free fall:** all structureless test bodies couple to one affine
   connection, independently of mass and composition;
2. **minimal coupling:** in the absence of nongravitational forces, no
   additional curvature-dependent force term is present;
3. **an affine parameter:** proper time in Lorentzian spacetime, and an
   affine parameter compatible with absolute time in Newton-Cartan spacetime.

The invariant equation is then

$$
u^\nu\nabla_\nu u^\lambda=0,
$$

or in coordinates,

$$
\frac{d^2x^\lambda}{d\lambda^2}
+\Gamma^\lambda_{\mu\nu}
\frac{dx^\mu}{d\lambda}
\frac{dx^\nu}{d\lambda}
=0.
$$

In general relativity, imposing torsion freedom and metric compatibility
makes $\Gamma$ the Levi-Civita connection of $g_{\mu\nu}$. In
Newton-Cartan theory, compatibility with the temporal metric and spatial
co-metric does not uniquely determine $\Gamma$; gravitational and Coriolis
data remain in the connection, as discussed in
[the Newton-Cartan note](16-nc.md).

At any event one can choose normal coordinates in which the symmetric part of
the connection vanishes at that event, reducing the equation there to
$d^2x^\lambda/d\lambda^2=0$. This is a pointwise statement. It does not make
the connection vanish on a neighborhood and does not imply zero curvature.

## Continuum conservation laws

For matter fields, conservation laws require a matter action and its field
equations. Diffeomorphism invariance of that action gives an on-shell Noether
identity.

In a Lorentzian theory with matter coupled only to the metric, the identity is

$$
\nabla_\mu T^{\mu\nu}=0.
$$

In Newton-Cartan theory the background data are degenerate and independent,
so the corresponding Ward identities involve the mass current, momentum
current, and energy current. They should not be compressed into the
Lorentzian formula without specifying the Newton-Cartan matter couplings.

These are consequences of the matter action's diffeomorphism invariance and
the matter equations of motion. They are not consequences of inertial-frame
symmetry alone, and deriving them does not require a gravitational field
equation.

## Dependency summary

| Result | Inputs actually used |
| --- | --- |
| $du/ds=0$ in both branches | local second-order law + translations + transitive velocity-space action + rotational stabilizer |
| $L_{\mathrm G}=\frac12m\mathbf v^2$ | local action + Galilean boost quasi-invariance + isotropy |
| $L_{\mathrm P}=-mc^2/\gamma$ | local additive Lorentz-scalar action + low-velocity matching |
| momentum and energy | established action + Noether definitions |
| geodesic motion | universal free fall + connection + minimal coupling |
| continuum Ward identities | diffeomorphism-invariant matter action + matter equations |

The non-circular chain is therefore not “symmetry fixes everything.”
The common spacetime symmetry structure fixes inertial motion within a stated
class; additional, explicit physical assumptions determine its
branch-specific variational representation, normalization, coupling to
gravity, and continuum conservation laws.
