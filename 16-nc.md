# Newton-Cartan

### Confirmed & Polished Summary of Type-I Newton–Cartan (NC)

**Common to both NC and GR**  
- A spacetime is described by a metric structure (a single non-degenerate metric in GR; a pair of degenerate metrics in NC) together with a connection.  
- In GR the metric uniquely determines the (Levi-Civita) connection.  
- In NC the degenerate metrics + compatibility conditions leave residual freedom: the only free connection components are  
  $$
  \Gamma^i_{00}\qquad\text{and}\qquad\Gamma^i_{0j}=-\Gamma^j_{0i}.
  $$
  To fully specify the geometry one must therefore give both the (flat) metrics *and* the free part of the connection.

**Visualization of NC spacetime**  
Absolute time slices are perfectly flat Euclidean 3-spaces (flat sheets of paper stacked along absolute time).  
The metrics $\tau_\mu$ and $h^{\mu\nu}$ tell you how to measure time between sheets and distances/angles on each sheet.  
They are not enough to decide how free particles move from one sheet to the next — that is the job of the independent connection.  
When gravity is present the connection bends the free-fall trajectories, so the spacetime is curved (non-zero Riemann tensor of the connection) even though every individual spatial sheet remains flat and time is absolute.

**The free connection components**  
- $\Gamma^i_{00}$ encodes Newtonian gravity. It is determined by the field equation  
  $$
  R_{\mu\nu}=4\pi G\rho\,\tau_\mu\tau_\nu.
  $$
- $\Gamma^i_{0j}$ encodes a possible rotational/Coriolis field.  
  Classical Newtonian physics assumes an absolute non-rotating frame exists, so we impose the Trautman condition  
  $$
  R^{[ab]}{}_{cd}=0
  $$
  (coordinate-invariant). This guarantees the existence of coordinates in which $\Gamma^i_{0j}=0$ everywhere.

**Rotating Earth example**  
In Earth-fixed coordinates both NC and GR have non-zero $\Gamma^i_{0j}$.  
Most of this is a pure coordinate effect of using a rotating frame.  
- In NC the Trautman condition guarantees we can always transform to a global non-rotating frame where $\Gamma^i_{0j}=0$.  
- In GR the Earth (or any rotating mass) also produces a small *intrinsic* frame-dragging (Lense-Thirring / gravitomagnetic) field. Even after going to the best non-rotating coordinates, a residual $\Gamma^i_{0j}$ (or $g_{0i}$) remains that cannot be gauged away.  
We deliberately exclude this effect in NC by imposing Trautman, so that the theory stays 100 % equivalent to classical Newtonian gravity.

**Axioms of classical Type-I NC**  
- **A1** Absolute structures: global absolute time + Euclidean geometry on each time slice  
  $\;\Rightarrow\;$ pair of degenerate *flat* metrics $(\tau_\mu,h^{\mu\nu})$.  
- **A2** Existence of a global non-rotating frame  
  $\;\Rightarrow\;$ Trautman condition $R^{[ab]}{}_{cd}=0$.  
- **A3** Field equation  
  $$
  R_{\mu\nu}=4\pi G\rho\,\tau_\mu\tau_\nu.
  $$

In the non-rotating frame given by A2 the only free connection components left are $\Gamma^i_{00}$, which are completely determined by A3.

**Crucial point (difference from GR)**  
The field equation A3 determines **only the connection**.  
It does *not* determine or curve the metrics.  
The metrics coming from A1 remain absolutely flat for all time; they are fixed background structures.  
Gravity curves only the independent connection that is compatible with those flat metrics.

---

This is precisely how classical Newtonian gravity is geometrised in a fully covariant, coordinate-independent way while remaining 100 % equivalent to the original theory.

# Newton-Cartan using a regulator metric

The regulator metric can be constructed entirely within Type-I Newton–Cartan theory. It is an auxiliary computational device, not a physical metric and not a solution of Einstein's equation. The logical order is:

1. specify the absolute Newton–Cartan structures;
2. use the Newton–Cartan field equation to solve for the connection;
3. package that already-known connection into a non-degenerate metric whose Levi-Civita connection is easy to compute.

### 1. Solve the Newton-Cartan field equation

The absolute structures $\tau_\mu$ and $h^{\mu\nu}$ are fixed by A1; they are not determined by the field equation. Choose adapted Cartesian coordinates for these flat structures,

$$
\tau=\mathrm{d}t,
\qquad
h^{ij}=\delta^{ij},
$$

and use the global non-rotating frame guaranteed by the Trautman condition. Metric compatibility and Trautman then reduce the undetermined part of the connection to

$$
\Gamma^i_{00}=\partial^i\Phi,
\qquad
\Gamma^i_{0j}=0.
$$

For this connection the only non-zero component of the Ricci tensor is

$$
R_{00}
=
\partial_i\Gamma^i_{00}
=
\delta^{ij}\partial_i\partial_j\Phi
=
\nabla^2\Phi.
$$

Consequently, the Newton–Cartan field equation

$$
R_{\mu\nu}
=
4\pi G\rho\,\tau_\mu\tau_\nu
$$

is precisely Poisson's equation,

$$
\nabla^2\Phi=4\pi G\rho.
$$

Time acts only as a parameter in this elliptic equation. For an isolated source, imposing $\Phi\to0$ at spatial infinity gives

$$
\Phi(t,\mathbf{x})
=
-G\int_{\mathbb{R}^3}
\frac{\rho(t,\mathbf{x}')}{|\mathbf{x}-\mathbf{x}'|}
\,\mathrm{d}^3x'.
$$

More general boundary conditions determine the corresponding harmonic contribution. Thus the source and boundary data determine $\Phi$, and hence the free connection component $\Gamma^i_{00}$. They do not determine $\tau_\mu$ or $h^{\mu\nu}$; those were supplied as absolute background structures.

### 2. Assemble the regulator metric

After solving for $\Phi$, choose the covariant spatial representative $h_{\mu\nu}$ associated with the adapted frame:

$$
h_{ij}=\delta_{ij},
\qquad
h_{00}=h_{0i}=0.
$$

Now define, for a formal parameter $c$,

$$
g^{(c)}_{\mu\nu}
=
h_{\mu\nu}
-
\bigl(c^2+2\Phi\bigr)\tau_\mu\tau_\nu.
$$

Equivalently,

$$
\mathrm{d}s_c^2
=
-\bigl(c^2+2\Phi(t,\mathbf{x})\bigr)\,\mathrm{d}t^2
+
\delta_{ij}\,\mathrm{d}x^i\mathrm{d}x^j.
$$

This formula is not guessed from GR. Its $-2\Phi\,\tau_\mu\tau_\nu$ term is chosen so that the Levi-Civita formula gives the Newton–Cartan component $\Gamma^i_{00}=\partial^i\Phi$ with the desired sign. The regulator metric is therefore assembled from:

- the fixed absolute data $\tau_\mu$ and $h^{\mu\nu}$;
- the potential $\Phi$ obtained from the Newton–Cartan field equation;
- the arbitrary formal parameter $c$.

In this qualified sense, the Newton–Cartan field equation determines the **gravitational part** of the regulator metric. It does not determine the complete metric by itself, because its absolute temporal and spatial parts remain prescribed background data.

### 3. Verify that it reproduces the Newton-Cartan connection

The inverse regulator metric in adapted coordinates is

$$
g_{(c)}^{00}
=
-\frac{1}{c^2+2\Phi},
\qquad
g_{(c)}^{0i}=0,
\qquad
g_{(c)}^{ij}=\delta^{ij}.
$$

Its Levi-Civita connection has

$$
\Gamma^i_{00}\bigl[g^{(c)}\bigr]
=
-\frac{1}{2}\delta^{ij}\partial_j g^{(c)}_{00}
=
\partial^i\Phi
$$

exactly, for every value of $c$. The additional components are

$$
\Gamma^0_{0i}\bigl[g^{(c)}\bigr]
=
\frac{\partial_i\Phi}{c^2+2\Phi},
\qquad
\Gamma^0_{00}\bigl[g^{(c)}\bigr]
=
\frac{\partial_t\Phi}{c^2+2\Phi},
$$

and therefore vanish as formal powers of $c^{-2}$. Thus

$$
\lim_{c\to\infty}
\Gamma^\lambda_{\mu\nu}\bigl[g^{(c)}\bigr]
=
\Gamma^\lambda_{\mu\nu}\bigl[\mathrm{NC}\bigr].
$$

The limit only removes the extra components introduced by making the degenerate Newton–Cartan structures artificially non-degenerate. It is not a non-relativistic limit of a GR solution.

### 4. Precise status of the construction

The finite-$c$ metric has no physical status in this use of the device. One does not impose Einstein's equation on it, interpret its light cones, or regard its finite-$c$ curvature as gravitational physics. Only the limiting connection is retained.

The regulator is also not unique. Terms of order $c^{-2}$ may be added without changing the limiting Newton–Cartan connection. Therefore the field equation plus the absolute structures determines a convenient regulator representative, not a unique physical metric.

| Input or operation | Status in Newton–Cartan theory |
|---|---|
| $\tau_\mu$, $h^{\mu\nu}$ | Fixed absolute structures |
| $\rho$ and boundary conditions | Physical source data |
| $\nabla^2\Phi=4\pi G\rho$ | Field equation determining the free connection |
| $g^{(c)}_{\mu\nu}=h_{\mu\nu}-(c^2+2\Phi)\tau_\mu\tau_\nu$ | Auxiliary packaging of the result |
| $c\to\infty$ | Formal removal of regulator artifacts |

Thus the construction remains firmly inside Type-I Newton–Cartan theory: the field equation first determines the gravitational connection, and the regulator metric is built afterwards as a convenient way to reproduce that connection using the Levi-Civita formula.

# A test planet orbiting a point-mass Sun

We now apply the same pipeline in Newton–Cartan theory and GR:

1. specify the source and symmetry;
2. solve the theory's field equation for its gravitational geometry;
3. compute the compatible connection;
4. solve the planet's geodesic equation.

In both theories the final equation of motion is

$$
\frac{\mathrm{d}^2x^\mu}{\mathrm{d}\lambda^2}
+
\Gamma^\mu_{\alpha\beta}
\frac{\mathrm{d}x^\alpha}{\mathrm{d}\lambda}
\frac{\mathrm{d}x^\beta}{\mathrm{d}\lambda}
=0.
$$

The Sun is a point mass $M$ fixed at the origin. The planet is a point mass $m$ in the limit $m/M\to0$, so it does not modify the Sun's field. This is therefore the test-mass limit, not the full two-body problem. Spherical symmetry lets us choose the orbital plane to be $\theta=\pi/2$ and use polar coordinates $(r,\phi)$.

To avoid confusing two different roles, $C$ will denote the formal Newton–Cartan regulator, while $c$ denotes the physical speed of light in GR.

## 1. Newton-Cartan theory

### Source and field equation

The point-mass density is

$$
\rho(\mathbf{x})=M\delta^{(3)}(\mathbf{x}).
$$

In adapted coordinates, the absolute structures are

$$
\tau=\mathrm{d}t,
\qquad
h^{\mu\nu}
=
\operatorname{diag}
\left(
0,\,
1,\,
\frac{1}{r^2},\,
\frac{1}{r^2\sin^2\theta}
\right).
$$

The Newton–Cartan field equation reduces to

$$
\nabla^2\Phi
=
4\pi GM\delta^{(3)}(\mathbf{x}).
$$

Asymptotic flatness, $\Phi\to0$ as $r\to\infty$, gives

$$
\Phi(r)=-\frac{GM}{r}.
$$

Thus the field equation determines the free part of the connection:

$$
\Gamma^r_{tt}
=
\partial_r\Phi
=
\frac{GM}{r^2}.
$$

### Metric structure and connection

Package the result into the auxiliary metric

$$
\mathrm{d}s_C^2
=
-\left(C^2-\frac{2GM}{r}\right)\mathrm{d}t^2
+
\mathrm{d}r^2
+
r^2\mathrm{d}\phi^2
$$

on the orbital plane. Its Levi-Civita connection has the components that survive as $C\to\infty$,

$$
\Gamma^r_{tt}=\frac{GM}{r^2},
\qquad
\Gamma^r_{\phi\phi}=-r,
\qquad
\Gamma^\phi_{r\phi}
=
\Gamma^\phi_{\phi r}
=
\frac{1}{r}.
$$

The first component is gravitational; the last two are the ordinary flat-space polar-coordinate connection.

### Geodesic and orbit

In the regulator limit, the time geodesic equation gives $\mathrm{d}^2t/\mathrm{d}\lambda^2=0$, so we may use absolute time $t$ as the affine parameter. The spatial geodesic equations are

$$
\ddot r-r\dot\phi^2+\frac{GM}{r^2}=0,
$$

$$
\ddot\phi+\frac{2}{r}\dot r\dot\phi=0,
$$

where dots denote derivatives with respect to $t$. The angular equation integrates to the conserved specific angular momentum

$$
\ell=r^2\dot\phi.
$$

Set $u(\phi)=1/r$. Eliminating $t$ from the radial equation gives the Binet equation

$$
\frac{\mathrm{d}^2u}{\mathrm{d}\phi^2}
+
u
=
\frac{GM}{\ell^2}.
$$

Its bound-orbit solution is

$$
u(\phi)
=
\frac{GM}{\ell^2}
\left[
1+e\cos(\phi-\phi_0)
\right],
$$

or

$$
r(\phi)
=
\frac{p}
{1+e\cos(\phi-\phi_0)},
\qquad
p=\frac{\ell^2}{GM}=a(1-e^2).
$$

The orbit is a closed ellipse with the Sun at one focus. Its period obeys

$$
T^2=\frac{4\pi^2}{GM}a^3.
$$

## 2. General relativity

### Source and field equation

The GR field equation is

$$
G_{\mu\nu}
=
\frac{8\pi G}{c^4}T_{\mu\nu}.
$$

Outside the point source, $T_{\mu\nu}=0$; the source fixes the total mass parameter $M$ through the boundary condition at spatial infinity.

### Metric structure and connection

Solving the field equation with these symmetry and boundary conditions gives the Schwarzschild metric. On the orbital plane,

$$
\mathrm{d}s^2
=
-f(r)c^2\mathrm{d}t^2
+
\frac{\mathrm{d}r^2}{f(r)}
+
r^2\mathrm{d}\phi^2,
\qquad
f(r)=1-\frac{2GM}{c^2r}.
$$

Here this is the physical spacetime metric, not a regulator. The idealized point source is represented by the Schwarzschild singularity and the mass parameter measured at spatial infinity.

The relevant Levi-Civita components are

$$
\Gamma^r_{tt}
=
f(r)\frac{GM}{r^2},
\qquad
\Gamma^r_{rr}
=
-\frac{GM}{c^2r^2f(r)},
$$

$$
\Gamma^r_{\phi\phi}
=
-f(r)r,
\qquad
\Gamma^\phi_{r\phi}
=
\Gamma^\phi_{\phi r}
=
\frac{1}{r},
\qquad
\Gamma^t_{tr}
=
\Gamma^t_{rt}
=
\frac{GM}{c^2r^2f(r)}.
$$

### Geodesic and orbit

Insert this connection into the same affine geodesic equation used above. Let $\tau_p$ be the planet's proper time, and let dots in this subsection denote derivatives with respect to $\tau_p$. The Killing symmetries give two conserved quantities per unit planet mass:

$$
\varepsilon
=
f(r)c^2\dot t,
\qquad
\ell
=
r^2\dot\phi.
$$

The timelike normalization

$$
g_{\mu\nu}\dot x^\mu\dot x^\nu=-c^2
$$

then gives the radial first integral

$$
\dot r^2
=
\frac{\varepsilon^2}{c^2}
-
f(r)
\left(
c^2+\frac{\ell^2}{r^2}
\right).
$$

Again set $u(\phi)=1/r$ and use $\dot\phi=\ell/r^2$. Differentiating the radial first integral produces the exact Schwarzschild orbit equation

$$
\frac{\mathrm{d}^2u}{\mathrm{d}\phi^2}
+
u
=
\frac{GM}{\ell^2}
+
\frac{3GM}{c^2}u^2.
$$

The first two terms are exactly the Newton–Cartan Binet equation. The additional term,

$$
\frac{3GM}{c^2}u^2,
$$

comes from the Schwarzschild metric and prevents a generic bound orbit from closing. To first order in $GM/(c^2p)$, the perihelion advance per radial period is

$$
\Delta\phi
=
\frac{6\pi GM}{c^2p}
=
\frac{6\pi GM}{c^2a(1-e^2)}.
$$

Thus the relativistic orbit is approximately

$$
r(\phi)
\approx
\frac{p}
{
1+e\cos\left[
\left(
1-\frac{3GM}{c^2p}
\right)
(\phi-\phi_0)
\right]
}.
$$

## 3. The common pipeline

| Stage | Newton–Cartan | General relativity |
|---|---|---|
| Source | $\rho=M\delta^{(3)}(\mathbf{x})$ | Point mass with Schwarzschild mass $M$ |
| Field equation | $R_{\mu\nu}=4\pi G\rho\,\tau_\mu\tau_\nu$ | $G_{\mu\nu}=8\pi G T_{\mu\nu}/c^4$ |
| Metric structure | Fixed $\tau_\mu,h^{\mu\nu}$ plus the solved connection, optionally packaged in $g^{(C)}_{\mu\nu}$ | Dynamical Schwarzschild metric $g_{\mu\nu}$ |
| Connection | Compatible NC connection, or $\lim_{C\to\infty}\Gamma[g^{(C)}]$ | Levi-Civita connection $\Gamma[g]$ |
| Motion | Affine geodesic equation | Affine geodesic equation |
| Polar orbit | $u''+u=GM/\ell^2$ | $u''+u=GM/\ell^2+3GMu^2/c^2$ |
| Bound-orbit result | Closed ellipse | Precessing ellipse |

The machinery after the geometry is obtained is the same: form the connection and solve the geodesic equation. The theories differ in what the field equation determines. Newton–Cartan theory keeps its degenerate temporal and spatial metrics fixed and solves for the remaining connection; GR solves for one non-degenerate spacetime metric, whose Levi-Civita connection is then fixed automatically.

# Selecting the dynamics from physical assumptions

Homogeneity, isotropy, and the relativity principle strongly constrain kinematics. Under the usual mild assumptions, the two physical possibilities are Galilean kinematics and Poincaré kinematics. These lead respectively to the degenerate Newtonian metric structure $(\tau_\mu,h^{\mu\nu})$ and the non-degenerate Lorentzian metric $g_{\mu\nu}$.

Symmetry alone, however, does not select a unique field equation. Infinitely many Galilean-invariant or Lorentz-invariant equations can be written. To obtain the classical Newton–Cartan and Einstein equations, one must supplement kinematics with assumptions about locality, field content, derivative order, and coupling to matter.

## 1. Common dynamical assumptions

The following assumptions can be imposed in both kinematic branches:

1. **Universal free fall:** all freely falling matter follows one gravitational connection, independently of its composition.
2. **Universal sourcing:** gravity couples to the conserved charge associated with the kinematics: mass density in Galilean spacetime and stress-energy in Lorentzian spacetime.
3. **Local covariance:** the equations are local and are written entirely in terms of the appropriate geometric structures and matter fields.
4. **Minimal gravitational field content:** no additional scalar, vector, or tensor gravitational fields are introduced.
5. **Second-order equations:** the field equations contain no more than two derivatives of the gravitational variables.
6. **No additional gravitational scale:** higher-derivative corrections and finite-range mass terms are excluded.
7. **Asymptotically flat vacuum:** the gravitational field approaches the appropriate flat geometry far from isolated sources.
8. **Empirical normalization:** inverse-square and free-fall experiments determine the sign and strength $G$ of the interaction.

These are simplicity and empirical assumptions, not consequences of spacetime symmetry alone.

## 2. Galilean kinematics selects Newton-Cartan dynamics

Universal free fall in Galilean spacetime is represented by an instantaneous gravitational potential $\Phi$ entering the connection as

$$
\Gamma^i_{00}=\partial^i\Phi.
$$

The equality of inertial, passive gravitational, and active gravitational mass implies that the same mass density $\rho$ which is conserved by matter dynamics also sources $\Phi$ and responds to it.

Now require:

- spatial locality and rotational invariance;
- invariance under $\Phi\mapsto\Phi+f(t)$, which leaves the gravitational acceleration unchanged;
- linear superposition of gravitational fields;
- at most two spatial derivatives;
- no propagating gravitational degree of freedom.

Up to normalization and boundary terms, the minimal action is then

$$
S_{\mathrm{NC}}
=
\int\mathrm{d}t\,\mathrm{d}^3x
\left[
-\frac{1}{8\pi G}
\delta^{ij}\partial_i\Phi\,\partial_j\Phi
-
\rho\Phi
\right].
$$

Varying $\Phi$ gives

$$
\nabla^2\Phi=4\pi G\rho.
$$

Since $\Gamma^i_{00}=\partial^i\Phi$, this is the adapted-coordinate form of

$$
R_{\mu\nu}
=
4\pi G\rho\,\tau_\mu\tau_\nu.
$$

Thus the classical Newton–Cartan field equation is the unique minimal local, linear, second-order equation for an instantaneous universally coupled gravitational potential. Asymptotic flatness removes an otherwise allowed source-independent constant term.

## 3. Poincare kinematics selects Einstein dynamics

Universal free fall in Lorentzian spacetime identifies the gravitational field with the metric $g_{\mu\nu}$. Matter couples universally to this metric, and its conserved source is the stress-energy tensor $T_{\mu\nu}$.

Require a local diffeomorphism-invariant action constructed only from $g_{\mu\nu}$ and producing second-order metric equations. In four dimensions, Lovelock's theorem implies that, up to boundary and topological terms, the only such gravitational action is

$$
S_{\mathrm{GR}}
=
\frac{c^3}{16\pi G}
\int\mathrm{d}^4x\,\sqrt{-g}\,(R-2\Lambda)
+
S_{\mathrm{matter}}[g,\psi].
$$

Varying $g_{\mu\nu}$ gives

$$
G_{\mu\nu}
+
\Lambda g_{\mu\nu}
=
\frac{8\pi G}{c^4}T_{\mu\nu}.
$$

The contracted Bianchi identity,

$$
\nabla^\mu G_{\mu\nu}=0,
$$

is consistent with the matter conservation law

$$
\nabla^\mu T_{\mu\nu}=0.
$$

Asymptotic flatness sets $\Lambda=0$, and the Newtonian weak-field limit fixes the coefficient $8\pi G/c^4$. The result is Einstein's equation in its simplest classical form.

There is also a complementary particle-physics derivation. Poincaré symmetry, locality, unitarity, and a single massless spin-2 field first give the linear Fierz–Pauli theory. Decoupling its unphysical polarizations requires a gauge symmetry. Requiring that the spin-2 field couple universally to stress-energy, including its own stress-energy, then iterates the linear theory into the nonlinear Einstein theory at two-derivative order.

## 4. Unified result

| Kinematics | Minimal gravitational variable | Conserved source | Minimal field equation |
|---|---|---|---|
| Galilean | Instantaneous scalar connection potential $\Phi$ | Mass density $\rho$ | $\nabla^2\Phi=4\pi G\rho$ |
| Poincaré | Massless spin-2 Lorentzian metric $g_{\mu\nu}$ | Stress-energy $T_{\mu\nu}$ | $G_{\mu\nu}=8\pi G T_{\mu\nu}/c^4$ |

The two classical theories therefore follow from the same general architecture:

$$
\text{kinematic symmetry}
+
\text{universal coupling}
+
\text{locality}
+
\text{minimal field content}
+
\text{second-order dynamics}.
$$

Relaxing these assumptions produces systematic extensions. Higher spatial derivatives, nonlinear potential terms, or propagating fields extend Newton–Cartan gravity; higher-curvature terms, additional fields, or extra dimensions extend GR. In effective-field-theory language, the classical NC and GR equations are the unique leading, lowest-derivative dynamics for their respective kinematic structures, while post-Newtonian and post-GR effects arise from controlled corrections.
